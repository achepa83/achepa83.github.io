Talk Strategy, vision & clarity

Especially with a team of highly skilled people, it is important to leave everyone enough freedom and room to unlock their full potential.
I want to micromanage ideation and planning, but not everyones work. Strategy, vision and requirements need to be absolutely clear.

If everyone knows and understands the "why?" and the "what?" with absolute clarity, great people will take care of the individual "hows?" and specialities of their field at their best.
Every minute spent for planning and clarifying in the beginning, can safe hours and days during execution.

Understand processes to build processes

I strongly believe that a leading role in a cross functional team should have a deep understanding of the individual processes of each team member.

Having an understanding of what it takes to create all sorts of content, build a new web page or a whole web application, is crutial to setting timelines, planning budgets, and making estimations.

Keeping timelines realistic and complexity as low as possible is also crutial for keeping everyone in the team healthy and sane...

Speak everyones language

Cross functional teams speak many different languages!
BUT! Musicologists don't speak sales or marketing. Sales doesn't speak content. Designers don't speak Code. Developers don't speak musicology.

A leader in such an environment should also be a translator and speak all of these languages well enough, to have meaningful conversations and get things done efficiently.

Developing a market segment

read more...

Problem

Stretta had neglected the wind music market since the beginning of their journey. They came from classical music and lacked the expertise in this field.
Wind music repertoire was badly to not at all curated in Stretta's database.
On top of that, Stretta's process of curating content was intentionally random to make sure that also unpopular and niche content was constantly curated and found it's way into the shop.

Insight

I knew that many wind orchestras easily spend 1.000€ and more for sheet music every year. There are around 16.000 wind bands in Germany alone, accounting for almost half of all ensembles in the country.

Solution

1. Fill the shop... fast!

Since we had another wind music expert in our team, I gave him the task to prepare the curation process, by collecting around 200 of the most important composers for wind music, and setting up new categories that conductors would like to quickly find, like:
Film music, Musical, Concert, Symphonic, Traditional (German) Folk Music, March, Waltz, Classical Transcriptions, Winds & Choir, Winds & Narrator, Brass Band, Youth Orchestra, Fanfare...

Now the rest of the product maintenance team could start curating every piece for these composers, and assigned our new categories to them.
We finished this process in about 8 weeks and had curated a few thousand wind music pieces, a good chunk of the top of the iceberg.

It would have taken years if not "forever" without this orchestrated two step effort.

2. Dedicated portal for wind music

I had the idea to build a dedicated new portal for wind music with specialized filter categories and easy access to different ensemble types like "Youth Orchestra" or "Brass Band".
Thinking like a conductor, I wanted to enable our customers to easily buy a whole new program of sheet music. A conductor may search for standards, easy works, some monumental concert works and every now and then something very special like a work with a choir or narrator.

3. Get the best podcaster in the field

At the same time I approached one of the most important podcaster in German speaking countries for wind music, and got him as our main influencer to talk about our new portal. His podcast was growing fast and his listeners where exclusively our exact target group of conductors and decision makers in this market segment.

I let Customers decide

How to navigate 1.000.000 items?

read more...

Problem

How should a menu be structured to navigate 1.000.000 items and hundreds of categories?

We had contradicting proposals. I was advocating a "mega menu" as the best choice in such a situation.
But we couldn't agree...

So, I proposed a Solution

Let our customers decide!

To settle our discussions and finally move things forward my team and I created four versions of the menu that where formerly proposed. Then we invited 50 customers with different backgrounds, amateurs, professionals, classical musicians, pop and jazz musicians, to vote and comment on each of the proposed menus.

Outcome:

We found a winner and tweaked it

The mega menu concept won. But thanks to our survey, we could further improve it and include feedback.
The final version was not just the original mega menu, but involved the best ideas from other concepts and from our customers.

How to work with influencers in the sheet music realm?

What you don't want to do

Find a famous guy with a large following and some regular posts. Pay him a lot of of money and then expect him to do, ehm, something with your sheet music.
Like, sitting at the pool in Buenos Aires and somehow praising your newest edition...

This feels unnatural. It feels awkward! It is awkward!!




In sales we call it the Jones effect


"People are more likely to be influenced by the actions and choices of those they perceive as similar to themselves."


It makes a company look like sitting in a very high, 300 years old ivory tower. Guess the company ;-)

Besides the costly unrelatability of such a choice, these types of "influencial people" are not professional content creators. This is a high-cost, low-outcome approach that, moreover, potentially damages how 'regular people' in the audience perceive a company.


What you rather want to do

Find professional creators in the music and music education realm and collaborate. Sponsor their existing formats. Treat them like the artist they are. What can we do for you? How about some freebies and goodies for your audience? We will take care of the shipping. Need anything else?


Creating win-win situations is key for thriving longterm relationships that benefit both sides.

Breitkopf was about to release a new Urtext edition of Richard Strauss's "An Alpine Symphony".
Nick Pfefferkorn himself was the editor. So I thought this could be a great opportunity do something special with viral potential for the global orchestra musicians community...

Insight

I am an orchestra low brass player. I know how much brass players freak out about Strauss's Alpine Symphony. A good story and well produced images around a new release of this piece could have gone viral in this community. And through the brass players it could reach orchestras allover the world.

Idea

Some of my former fellow students and friends are holding positions in Germanys finest orchestras. They founded "Salaputia Brass" about ten years ago. Salaputia gathers Germanys finest next generation of orchestra brass players. They can truly be compared to German Brass.
I wanted to invite these guys to do a mountain tour in the alps and play an arrangement of "On the summit" on an actual summit. We would hire a small film team, get the hero shot on the mountain top with a drone orbiting the ensemble and make a beautiful video of this alpine happening.
Besides that, we would produce a behind the scenes video, showing the "band of brothers", the planning and the exhausting tour to the summit, with all the instruments and equipment that need to be carried.

Possible outcome

This could have been a viral hit, if produced well and seeded throughout platforms, magazines and blogs allover the world. You don't just post something like this on your own channels and wait for magic to happen. You need to actively boost and orchestrate such a release in advance.

It would have given Breitkopf a juvenile and adventurous attribute. Something that this 300 years old man urgently needs.

Costs

I wanted to get the ensemble (almost) for free. We would just pay for the travel. A good two or three man film crew could be hired locally for a few thousand euros for two days. A good editor on top and we could have done this for around 10.000€.
That's not a lot of money for a possible global impact in a highly focussed target group and an image refresh.
The ensemble would have had a great trip and global marketing for free. It should have been a no brainer for them.

Nick actually loved the idea a lot. But, it never happened...

Turning weakness into strength

read more...

Would you expect musicologists to write engaging content?

Musicologists are scientists. Besides some naturally talented individuals, they would almost always write scientific texts. They can argue for hours about the correct type of quotation marks. But their content often reads like a Wikipedia article. Names, places, dates, works and more names, places...

I had to nurture my team

My mission was clear. It was my job to enable my team to write better, more engaging content and copy. So I collected resources about blog structures, storytelling, how to write a report, a commentary and what type of language to use and not to use.
I encouraged them to try new approaches and find their creative voice. It was about engaging a wide audience and not about impressing other experts out there.

Copy guidelines across teams and touch points

I quickly realized that Stretta needed a unified tone of voice for any kind of copy. Not just long form texts in Stretta's Journal. So I developed a set of simple guidelines to help streamline copy across any possible touch points: social media, customer service, newsletter, website system text, invoices...
Guides like:

  • Use a simple, casual language
  • Don't try to sound smart
  • Keep it on eye level
  • If you have to choose, use a simple word instead of a sophisticated word
  • Keep most of your sentences short without nesting
  • Vary the length of your sentences
  • Use "we" when you refer to Stretta more often
  • Remember that the natural flow of a sentence is: subject, predicate, object (at least in German)
  • Read your copy out loud! Does it sound natural to yourself?

    • ...

Such simple recommendations can produce "in sum" a more unified tone that caters to a wider audience.

Finally

This work payed off after a while. My proof reading was focussing more and more just on little typos. And I didn't have to work through a text or piece of copy anymore, and explain sentence by sentence how it might be better for the web...

One of my younger team members, who was still studying, told me later that his marks for his research papers got better, and that one of his professors noticed a change, for the better, in his writing.
I liked that!
